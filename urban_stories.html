WORK FROM HOME (WFH)

Motivation for the project:

Since the Pandemic, workplace mobility in the UK remains below pre-pandemic levels. This chart motivated this project as it showcases that cities are at a 'tipping point' if we maintain a form of WFH. This project explores the economic benefits and costs of cities as cities face a potential decline.

Google mobility data.

Google publishes Community Mobility reports as pdfs, so using this Kaggle dataset is more efficient. The data is smoothed using locally estimated scatterplot smoothing (Loess) function to adjust for daily fluctuations.

EFFICIENCY

Click on the legend to select country development.

Unrestricted vs Restricted regressions.

These charts use a restricted and unrestricted regression to show emissions per capita on population density. Cities in the most developed regions (MDR) show a negative relationship indicating that denser cities are more efficient. However, there is an insignificant relationship between density and emissions per capita for cities in less developed countries (LDC), suggesting there are no efficiency gains for cities in these countries. This could be due to other factors such as the share of consumption on services. As the restricted regressions are statistically different from the unrestricted regressions it is best to use the chart with cities from MDR.

Efficiency chart notes.

The regressions use a polynomial function as spillover effects may cause a non-linear relationship between the variables. This relationship holds in the regression on MDR cities although the R-Squared is only 49%. Population density is also preferred over total population because this accounts for differences in land area.

INNOVATION

Knowledge spill over effects.

Cities are a hotbed for innovation.

Higher deciles of population density had higher patents per 10,000 people in the years 2010-15. The later period for patent data rules out reverse causality between the variables. There are two outliers in the first and third deciles. This could be because some companies have larger campuses in the least dense cities. E.g., Micron Technologies is based in Boise-Idaho, in the first decile. Technologies such as videoconferencing challenge the benefits of knowledge spill overs because workers do not need to locate in cities to share ideas.

Chart notes.

Patent data was scrapped from the US Patent Office website and then merged with US census density data. Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSAs) are large urban agglomerations in the US used to define large cities. As countries have few large cities, city data can be highly skewed. Therefore, cities are grouped into deciles rather than removing outliers.

WAGES

Outcome of multiple linear regression model

A model to predict wages.

Average wages are statistically significant higher in MSAs than in micropolitan cities (MIC), indicating that wages are higher in larger cities. To investigate this, I used a multiple linear regression on all MSA's and split the sample 70:30 into a training and test sample. The outcome variable is the ln(avg. wages) and the independent variables are human capital, population size and per capita GDP. Surprisingly, only the population share with at least a bachelor's degree has a negative coefficient, -0.001 however the magnitude may make it economically insignificant. The chart shows the model's outcome with the predicted values of the outcome variable against the test values. The model predicts that cities with larger populations and higher GDP per capita lead to higher average wages.

Dataset notes.

The share of the population with at least a bachelor's degree comes from The State Science & Technology Institute (SSTI). It is the only variable not to be pulled from the Bureau of Economic Analysis (Bea) API. A loop cleans each API's data and then merges them into a data frame. Average wages are adjusted for price levels by dividing them by the regional price parity index.

OUTPUT

Cities are major contributors to national GDP.

Large cities are huge contributors to countries national income relative to their share of the national population. Cities with larger populations as a share of their countries are always left of the 45-degree curve.

Dataset notes.

The data comes from the OECD API, which limits large requests and reduces the sample size. The data covers 18 years to show changes in cities share of GDP and share of the country's population over time. Although these variables have slight variation over time, there is a noticeable dip in the share of GDP during the 2008-09 recession.

INFECTIOUS DISEASE

Simple linear regression model to show that countries where a higher share of the population live in urban areas had a higher incidence of Covid-19.

Cities spread diseases.

Countries with a higher urban population also had higher Covid-19 cases. This simple regression is statistically significant even though factors like age are excluded. Countries in developed continents are urbanised and have the highest number of Covid-19 cases compared to African countries with smaller urban population and lower total cases. However, there could be inconsistency in testing for Covid-19. The Covid-19 data comes from the Our World in Data (OWID) GitHub repository instead of through an API for simplicity.

LONG-RUN TRENDS

Conclusions.

Therefore, it is likely the tipping point of cities will be in developed rather than developing countries. However, cities are resilient and will survive the Pandemic even if WFH remains popular. Potential extensions to this project include studying the effects of WFH on migration out of cities and if there will be a post-pandemic decline in innovation in cities due to WFH.
